# Service Frequency Analysis
This analysis examines the frequency and distribution of knotless braid services across the sampled providers. The goal is to understand how consistently these services appear, how they vary across providers, and whether they form a stable and repeatable service pattern.
The dataset includes 32 knotless braid service records across 7 providers, all based in London. This is a deliberately small and focused sample, designed to demonstrate the method rather than represent the full UK market (avalible upon request).

# Service Presence Across Providers and Variation Within Knotless Services
Knotless braids appear across all providers in the dataset, often with multiple variations offered by the same stylist indicating that knotless braids are a core repeatable offering within the afro hair services market.  Although all services fall under the same family, there is clear variation in how they are offered and described. The majority of services fall under a standard knotless format, with 19/32 services classified as standard. More stylised variations such as goddess or curly knotless braids appear in 7 cases, while other variants such as fulani, frontal, and boho styles appear less frequently. This suggests that while variation exists, the market is anchored around a consistent base service with optional stylistic enhancements layered on top.

# Size and Length Variation
Size is one of the most consistently specified attributes across services. The dataset shows a relatively even distribution between large and medium sizes, with 10 large and 9 medium services, followed by smaller counts of small, small-medium, and jumbo styles. A small number of services do not specify size explicitly. This indicates that size is a key dimension of service definition and is likely tied to both pricing and customer preference. The consistency of size-based variation also reinforces the structured nature of the service. Length is less consistently defined than size. Out of the 32 services, 18 do not specify length, while the remaining services include descriptors such as mid-back (7), waist (4), and occasional references to bob, short, or bum length. This suggests that while length is important, it is not always standardised in naming conventions.

# Service Modifiers
Two modifiers appear within the dataset that indicate additional service dynamics. There are 2 refresh services, which represent maintenance rather than full installation. This is important because it introduces the idea of a service lifecycle rather than a one-time transaction.
There are also 4 services explicitly targeted at children, indicating a distinct sub-segment with potentially different pricing, time requirements, and product usage.

# Key Observations
The most important observation from this dataset is the level of structural consistency. Knotless braids appear across all providers, with repeatable patterns in how services are defined and offered. Even though naming conventions vary, the underlying structure remains consistent. Services are typically composed of a base type (knotless braids), modified by size, and optionally enhanced with stylistic elements such as curls or specific patterns. Another important observation is that variation exists within a controlled range. While there are multiple style variants, the majority of services remain close to a standard format. This balance between consistency and variation makes the dataset suitable for aggregation and comparison.
Finally, the presence of refresh services indicates that demand is ongoing rather than one-off. This suggests that the services being analysed are part of a broader maintenance cycle, which has implications for recurring product usage.

# Implications for Product Demand
The analysis shows that knotless braids are a high-frequency, structurally embedded service within the sampled market. The consistency of the service structure suggests that the underlying workflows required to deliver these services are also consistent. This is important because it means that any products required to support these workflows are likely to represent repeat, reliable demand rather than trend-driven demand. This provides a strong foundation for the next stage of the analysis, where these services will be mapped to the products required to deliver them.
